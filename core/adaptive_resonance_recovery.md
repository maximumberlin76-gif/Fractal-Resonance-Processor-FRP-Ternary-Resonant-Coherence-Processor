# Adaptive Resonance Recovery

This document describes the adaptive resonance-recovery mechanisms used within the Fractal Resonance Processor (FPR) framework.

## 1. Recovery Principle

Within the FPR framework, coherent computational structures may experience partial synchronization degradation under nonlinear dissipative conditions.

The processor therefore attempts not only to prevent collapse, but also to restore stable coherent phase interaction after destabilization events.

## 2. Recovery Dynamics

Adaptive recovery operates through:

- recursive phase-coherence restabilization,
- resonance compatibility reevaluation,
- synchronization-path reconstruction,
- coherence-retention reinforcement,
- and dissipative fragmentation suppression.

The recovery process continuously evaluates the qualitative characteristics of phase-coherence destabilization dynamics.

## 3. Transitional Recovery States

The framework distinguishes transitional recovery states from fully stabilized coherent-restoration regimes.

Transitional recovery states may contain:

- partially restored synchronization regions,
- unstable coherence-retention conditions,
- fluctuating resonance compatibility,
- and dynamically evolving phase-coherence structures.

These states may either stabilize into coherent retention or collapse into renewed fragmentation.

## 4. Recursive Recovery Reinforcement

Recovered synchronization structures recursively reinforce neighboring coherent regions.

This recursive reinforcement may increase:

- synchronization persistence,
- coherence-retention stability,
- resonance-domain recovery capability,
- and large-scale coherent-restoration propagation.

## 5. Dissipative Recovery Failure

If dissipative fragmentation continues to exceed coherence accumulation during recovery, restoration progressively destabilizes.

This may produce:

- repeated synchronization collapse,
- recursive fragmentation amplification,
- unstable phase divergence,
- and failure of coherent-retention restoration.

## 6. Dynamic Stability Condition

Stable adaptive resonance recovery remains possible only while:

`C(t) > P(t)`

across interacting synchronization domains and coherence-retention structures.

## 7. FPR Interpretation

Within the FPR framework, adaptive resonance recovery is interpreted as a recursive resonance-driven phase-coherence restoration process operating through nonlinear synchronization stabilization and distributed coherent-retention reconstruction dynamics.
