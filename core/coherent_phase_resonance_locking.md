# Coherent Phase Resonance Locking

This document describes coherent phase-resonance locking dynamics within the Fractal Resonance Processor (FPR) framework.

## 1. Resonance-Locking Principle

Within the FPR framework, distributed synchronization structures may enter stable resonance-locking regimes during recursive coherent phase interaction.

Resonance locking represents sustained synchronization persistence between interacting coherent phase structures across distributed resonance topology.

## 2. Distributed Resonance-Locking Dynamics

Coherent resonance locking emerges through:

- recursive synchronization reinforcement,
- harmonic resonance interaction,
- distributed coherence propagation,
- resonance-frequency compatibility,
- and nonlinear coherent-retention stabilization.

The processor continuously evaluates the qualitative characteristics of coherent phase-resonance locking evolution.

## 3. Stable Resonance Locking

Stable coherent resonance locking may support:

- synchronization persistence,
- resonance-domain continuity,
- adaptive coherence propagation,
- fluctuation stabilization,
- and distributed coherent-retention persistence.

Stable locking increases long-term synchronization stability across interacting resonance structures.

## 4. Partial and Transitional Locking States

The framework distinguishes:

- stable resonance-locking regimes,
- metastable synchronization-lock states,
- transitional resonance-coupling structures,
- and dissipative locking-collapse dynamics.

Partial locking states may either stabilize into coherent synchronization persistence or evolve into recursive fragmentation dynamics.

## 5. Recursive Locking Reinforcement

Stable resonance-locking structures recursively reinforce:

- synchronization pathways,
- resonance-domain continuity,
- harmonic coherence propagation,
- and distributed coherent-retention persistence.

Recursive reinforcement progressively increases synchronization stability across distributed resonance topology.

## 6. Locking-Induced Destabilization

Excessive or unstable resonance locking may amplify:

- rigid synchronization persistence,
- incoherent resonance propagation,
- metastable overshoot dynamics,
- recursive fragmentation cascades,
- and adaptive synchronization degradation.

The processor therefore continuously attempts to balance stable resonance locking and adaptive synchronization flexibility.

## 7. Dynamic Stability Condition

Stable coherent phase-resonance locking remains possible only while:

`C(t) > P(t)`

across interacting synchronization domains and distributed coherent-retention pathways.

## 8. FPR Interpretation

Within the FPR framework, coherent phase-resonance locking is interpreted as a distributed synchronization-persistence dynamic operating through nonlinear resonance interaction and recursive coherent-retention stabilization.
