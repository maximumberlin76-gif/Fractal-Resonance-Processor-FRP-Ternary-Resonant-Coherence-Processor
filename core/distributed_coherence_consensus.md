# Distributed Coherence Consensus

This document describes the distributed coherence-consensus mechanisms used within the Fractal Resonance Processor (FPR) framework.

## 1. Consensus Principle

Within the FPR framework, large-scale coherent computation requires distributed agreement between interacting resonance domains.

The processor therefore continuously evaluates coherent phase compatibility across distributed synchronization structures.

## 2. Distributed Consensus Dynamics

Consensus formation operates through recursive interaction between:

- resonance domains,
- synchronization regions,
- coherence-retention structures,
- and distributed phase-coherence pathways.

The processor evaluates the qualitative characteristics of coherent phase interaction dynamics across distributed structures.

## 3. Coherent Consensus Formation

Distributed coherence consensus emerges through:

- sustained phase synchronization compatibility,
- recursive coherence reinforcement,
- resonance-retention persistence,
- and stabilization of distributed coherent interaction pathways.

Stable consensus progressively increases large-scale coherent-retention capability.

## 4. Transitional Consensus States

The framework distinguishes transitional consensus states from fully stabilized distributed coherent-consensus regimes.

Transitional states may contain:

- partially synchronized resonance domains,
- unstable coherence-retention pathways,
- fluctuating phase-coherence compatibility,
- and dynamically evolving synchronization structures.

These states may either stabilize or collapse into distributed fragmentation.

## 5. Recursive Consensus Reinforcement

Stabilized consensus structures recursively reinforce:

- neighboring synchronization regions,
- distributed coherence pathways,
- resonance-domain persistence,
- and large-scale coherent-retention stability.

This recursive reinforcement improves long-term distributed synchronization persistence.

## 6. Dissipative Consensus Collapse

If dissipative fragmentation exceeds coherence accumulation across distributed structures, consensus progressively destabilizes.

This may produce:

- distributed synchronization divergence,
- coherence fragmentation propagation,
- unstable phase drift amplification,
- and collapse of coherent-retention persistence.

## 7. Dynamic Stability Condition

Stable distributed coherence consensus remains possible only while:

`C(t) > P(t)`

across interacting synchronization domains and distributed coherent-retention structures.

## 8. FPR Interpretation

Within the FPR framework, distributed coherence consensus is interpreted as a recursive resonance-driven phase-coherence agreement mechanism operating through nonlinear synchronization dynamics and distributed coherent-retention stabilization.
