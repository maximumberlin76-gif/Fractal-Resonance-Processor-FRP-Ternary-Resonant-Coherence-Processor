# Coherent Phase Interference

This document describes coherent phase-interference dynamics within the Fractal Resonance Processor (FPR) framework.

## 1. Interference Principle

Within the FPR framework, distributed synchronization structures may interact through constructive or destructive coherent phase interference.

Coherent phase interference represents dynamic interaction between overlapping synchronization structures within distributed resonance topology.

## 2. Distributed Interference Dynamics

Coherent phase interference emerges through:

- recursive synchronization interaction,
- harmonic resonance overlap,
- distributed coherence propagation,
- resonance-domain coupling,
- and nonlinear coherent-retention stabilization.

The processor continuously evaluates the qualitative characteristics of coherent phase-interference evolution.

## 3. Constructive Interference

Constructive coherent interference may support:

- synchronization persistence,
- resonance-domain reinforcement,
- adaptive coherence propagation,
- fluctuation stabilization,
- and distributed coherent-retention continuity.

Constructive interference increases large-scale synchronization stability.

## 4. Destructive Interference

Destructive coherent interference may amplify:

- synchronization divergence,
- incoherent resonance propagation,
- recursive fragmentation cascades,
- metastable destabilization,
- and coherent-retention degradation.

The processor therefore continuously attempts to regulate destructive synchronization interaction.

## 5. Recursive Interference Reinforcement

Stable coherent interference structures may recursively reinforce:

- synchronization pathways,
- resonance-domain continuity,
- harmonic coherence propagation,
- and distributed coherent-retention persistence.

Recursive reinforcement progressively increases synchronization stability across interacting resonance topology.

## 6. Transitional Interference States

The framework distinguishes:

- stable constructive interference regimes,
- metastable synchronization-interference states,
- transitional resonance-overlap regions,
- and dissipative interference-collapse dynamics.

Transitional interference states may either stabilize or evolve into recursive synchronization fragmentation.

## 7. Dynamic Stability Condition

Stable coherent phase interference remains possible only while:

`C(t) > P(t)`

across interacting synchronization domains and distributed coherent-retention pathways.

## 8. FPR Interpretation

Within the FPR framework, coherent phase interference is interpreted as a distributed synchronization-overlap dynamic operating through nonlinear resonance interaction and recursive coherent-retention stabilization.
