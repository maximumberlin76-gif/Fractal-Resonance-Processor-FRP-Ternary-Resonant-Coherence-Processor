# Coherent Time Dynamics

This document describes the role of time in coherent phase retention within the Fractal Resonance Processor (FPR) framework.

## 1. Temporal Coherence Principle

Within the FPR framework, coherent computation is not defined by a static state alone.

A coherent computational state must remain dynamically stable over time through sustained phase synchronization and coherent-retention persistence.

## 2. Time-Dependent Coherence Retention

Coherence retention depends on:

- duration of coherent phase-lock stability,
- persistence of phase synchronization,
- fluctuation resistance over time,
- resonance compatibility stability,
- and resistance to dissipative fragmentation dynamics.

## 3. Coherence Half-Life

The framework may evaluate coherence half-life as the time interval during which a coherent phase structure retains a significant portion of its synchronization stability.

Coherence half-life can be used to compare:

- stable coherent regimes,
- metastable resonance regimes,
- transitional phase states,
- and dissipative fragmentation trajectories.

## 4. Recursive Temporal Stabilization

Stable coherent regimes may reinforce themselves over repeated processing cycles.

This recursive temporal stabilization may increase:

- coherent-retention duration,
- phase synchronization persistence,
- resonance-domain stability,
- and resistance to fluctuation-driven degradation.

## 5. Temporal Collapse Dynamics

If dissipative fragmentation exceeds coherence accumulation over time, coherent retention progressively degrades.

This may produce:

- phase synchronization decay,
- coherence-retention collapse,
- unstable phase drift,
- and loss of coherent computational persistence.

## 6. Dynamic Stability Condition

Stable coherent time dynamics remain possible only while:

`C(t) > P(t)`

over the relevant processing interval.

## 7. FPR Interpretation

Within the FPR framework, coherent time dynamics are interpreted as sustained phase-coherence preservation over time through recursive resonance interaction, nonlinear phase synchronization, and coherent-retention stabilization.
