# Coherent Phase Momentum

This document describes coherent phase-momentum dynamics within the Fractal Resonance Processor (FPR) framework.

## 1. Phase-Momentum Principle

Within the FPR framework, coherent synchronization propagation may accumulate directional stabilization momentum across distributed resonance structures.

Phase momentum represents the accumulated tendency of coherent synchronization flow to preserve propagation direction and coherent-retention persistence over time.

## 2. Distributed Momentum Dynamics

Coherent phase momentum emerges through:

- recursive synchronization reinforcement,
- distributed coherence propagation,
- phase-gradient persistence,
- resonance-domain continuity,
- and nonlinear coherent-retention stabilization.

The processor continuously evaluates the qualitative characteristics of coherent phase-momentum evolution.

## 3. Momentum and Synchronization Stability

Stable coherent phase momentum may support:

- synchronization persistence,
- directional coherence propagation,
- resonance-domain continuity,
- fluctuation resistance,
- and adaptive coherent-retention stabilization.

Weak momentum may increase vulnerability to dissipative fragmentation dynamics.

## 4. Recursive Momentum Reinforcement

The processor recursively reinforces coherent phase momentum through:

- synchronization arbitration,
- adaptive phase-routing stabilization,
- recursive coherence inheritance,
- resonance-memory persistence,
- and distributed coherent-retention support.

Recursive reinforcement progressively increases large-scale synchronization persistence capability.

## 5. Transitional Momentum States

The framework distinguishes:

- stable coherent momentum regimes,
- metastable synchronization-propagation states,
- transitional resonance-flow structures,
- and dissipative momentum-collapse dynamics.

Transitional momentum states may either stabilize or evolve into recursive synchronization fragmentation.

## 6. Momentum-Induced Destabilization

Excessive coherent phase momentum may destabilize adaptive synchronization flexibility.

This may produce:

- rigid resonance propagation,
- unstable synchronization persistence,
- recursive coherence overshoot,
- and delayed fragmentation collapse dynamics.

The processor therefore continuously attempts to balance synchronization persistence and adaptive resonance correction capability.

## 7. Dynamic Stability Condition

Stable coherent phase momentum remains possible only while:

`C(t) > P(t)`

across interacting synchronization domains and distributed coherent-retention pathways.

## 8. FPR Interpretation

Within the FPR framework, coherent phase momentum is interpreted as a distributed synchronization-propagation persistence dynamic operating through nonlinear resonance interaction and recursive coherent-retention stabilization.
