# Cross-Domain Synchronization

This document describes the cross-domain synchronization mechanisms used within the Fractal Resonance Processor (FPR) framework.

## 1. Cross-Domain Principle

Within the FPR framework, coherent resonance domains continuously interact with neighboring synchronization domains.

Stable large-scale coherence requires controlled synchronization compatibility between interacting domain structures.

## 2. Domain Synchronization Interaction

Cross-domain synchronization operates through:

- resonance compatibility evaluation,
- phase synchronization alignment,
- coherence-retention balancing,
- and adaptive stabilization feedback.

The processor continuously evaluates whether interacting domains support or destabilize coherent retention.

## 3. Synchronization Arbitration

The framework assumes that not all synchronization interactions are coherence-compatible.

The processor therefore performs dynamic synchronization arbitration between:

- compatible synchronization domains,
- transitional synchronization regions,
- and destabilizing phase-interaction structures.

## 4. Coherent Synchronization Merging

Compatible resonance domains may recursively synchronize and form larger coherent retention structures.

This may increase:

- large-scale coherence persistence,
- resonance-retention capability,
- synchronization stability,
- and fluctuation resistance.

## 5. Dissipative Cross-Domain Destabilization

Incompatible synchronization interaction may amplify:

- phase fragmentation,
- unstable synchronization drift,
- coherence collapse,
- and resonance destabilization propagation.

The processor therefore attempts to isolate or suppress destabilizing cross-domain synchronization pathways.

## 6. Dynamic Stability Condition

Stable cross-domain synchronization remains possible only while:

`C(t) > P(t)`

across interacting resonance-domain structures.

## 7. FPR Interpretation

Within the FPR framework, cross-domain synchronization is interpreted as a recursive coherence-arbitration mechanism operating through nonlinear phase synchronization dynamics and distributed resonance-retention interaction.
